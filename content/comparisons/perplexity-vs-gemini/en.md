[Perplexity](/en/articles/perplexity) challenges the very reason Google Search exists — an "answer engine" that returns a cited answer instead of a list of links. And yet Perplexity's own API documentation lists, among its routing destinations, the name of the giant it's challenging: Google.

## Selling the model of the company you're trying to break

:::fact
Per the [Perplexity](/en/articles/perplexity) dissection, its official API documentation states the Agent API can route third-party models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and xAI "with no markup," at the providers' own rates. Per the [Gemini](/en/articles/gemini) dissection, Google publishes its own model lineup — Gemini 3.6 Flash, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and more — on Google DeepMind's official page.
:::

A Perplexity user comes to Perplexity precisely to reach information without going through Google Search. And yet, underneath the answer it generates, that same user can choose to run the query on a model Google itself built. Rivalry and dependency coexist inside the same product.

:::pull
The company trying to break the reason search exists stocks the model of the company it's breaking on its own shelf. Less a rivalry than two companies occupying different layers of the same arena.
:::

:::guess
This suggests that for Perplexity, "which AI model to use" and "how to deliver information" are two separate contests. On raw model capability, folding in the best available third-party models alongside its own Sonar makes for a stronger product, and there's no reason to exclude Google from that list. What Perplexity is actually fighting Google over is the delivery layer — cited answers, an ad-free search experience. That double structure, cooperating technically while competing experientially, shows that rivalry between AI companies doesn't reduce to simple hostility.
:::

## Building distribution from zero, versus already owning it

In the fight over where information gets delivered, the two companies' positions are asymmetric.

:::fact
Per the [Perplexity](/en/articles/perplexity) dissection, its Chromium-based browser, Comet, launched subscription-exclusive in July 2025 and opened up for free that October. Per the [Gemini](/en/articles/gemini) dissection, Gemini is already embedded across Google Search, Workspace (Gmail/Docs/Sheets), Android, Chrome, Google Photos, and Messages, and serves as the default assistant on Pixel and Galaxy devices.
:::

:::guess
Perplexity having to build a browser — an entry point — from scratch likely reflects being an independent company with no search engine, OS, or device to serve as distribution. Comet plausibly represents the only realistic path for a later entrant to claim the asset Google already owns: the screen a user opens first. Gemini, by contrast, as part of the "owns its own chips and distribution" structure described in the [Gemini dissection](/en/articles/gemini), reaches billions of people just by adding features to existing products, with no need to build a new app at all. Perplexity is trying to close that gap through capital raised as an independent company — a $21.2 billion valuation as of early 2026 — while Gemini can draw on parent Alphabet's existing distribution and balance sheet. Within the same AI race, the kind of investment each side needs is fundamentally different.
:::

Perplexity uses the model of the company it's trying to break as part of its own product, while having to build, entirely on its own, the one thing that company already owns: distribution. The mechanical comparison's zero shared technology isn't a coincidence — it reflects the two companies occupying different positions on different layers, models and distribution, within the same market. What this dissection of Perplexity and Gemini reveals is a rivalry between AI companies that carries technical cooperation and experiential competition at the same time — something too complicated for simple hostility to explain.
