[Temu](/en/articles/temu) and [Whatnot](/en/articles/whatnot) both tackle the oldest problem in online commerce: buying something from a stranger. And yet their answers are close to opposites.

## Temu erases the human; Whatnot puts them front and center

:::fact
Per the [Temu](/en/articles/temu) dissection, Temu runs a "next-gen manufacturing" model where China-based sellers ship directly to consumers with no intermediate distributor, and sellers must clear a bar of 30 units and $90 in sales within 14 days. Individual sellers' faces or personalities stay in the background; overwhelming price and a mountain of sales volume stand in for trust instead. Per the [Whatnot](/en/articles/whatnot) dissection, Whatnot's core mechanism has sellers present items one at a time on a live broadcast while narrating, and viewers verify condition in real time before bidding or buying.
:::

:::pull
Temu builds trust by erasing who's selling. Whatnot builds trust by showing you exactly who's selling. Two opposite answers to the same anxiety about buying from a stranger.
:::

:::guess
This difference likely traces back to what kind of goods each platform sells. The everyday goods Temu carries have small unit-to-unit variation and standardize easily, so statistical trust — a mountain of sales volume — functions well enough on its own. The trading cards and collectibles Whatnot carries have condition variation that directly determines price, so information that photos and text alone can't fully convey needs the livestream format to fill the gap. Whether you erase the human or foreground them looks less like a philosophical choice and more like a consequence of what kind of inventory you're actually selling — mass-produced, standardized goods, or unique items where condition matters enormously.
:::

## Expansion forced by regulation, versus expansion chosen freely

Both companies are trying to extend their original winning pattern into new territory. But what's driving that extension couldn't be more different.

:::fact
Per the [Temu](/en/articles/temu) dissection, in May 2025, following a change in US tariff policy (a revision to the low-value-import duty exemption), Temu was forced to drop direct shipping from China in favor of a model where locally based sellers hold inventory and ship within the US. Per the [Whatnot](/en/articles/whatnot) dissection, Whatnot expanded on its own initiative beyond collectibles — into shelf-stable food in summer 2025 and fresh food in April 2026.
:::

:::guess
Temu's operational pivot was a passive response forced by an external regulatory change. Whatnot's expansion into food, by contrast, looks like an active choice grounded in confidence about the "show it live, sell it live" experience it refined in collectibles — arguably the hardest category to build trust in. As it happens, both companies find themselves testing, at roughly the same time, whether their business holds up outside its original assumptions: Temu outside the duty exemption it was built on, Whatnot outside the single category it started with. For Temu, this is a forced experiment for survival; for Whatnot, a chosen experiment for growth. Different as those motivations are, both companies face the same underlying question — how far the winning pattern they found first can actually generalize.
:::

Faced with the same anxiety about buying from a stranger, Temu answered by erasing the human, and Whatnot by showing them clearly. Temu, pushed by regulation into localizing its supply chain, and Whatnot, choosing on its own to expand into new categories — different motivations, but both now testing whether the winning pattern they found first holds up outside the conditions that first produced it. Overlaying the two dissections reveals a structure where how you build trust also shapes how growth eventually gets forced on you.
